\chapter{Project Overview \& Objectives}
% ============================================================

\section{Assignment Context}

This report documents the collaborative low-fidelity prototype design
process for the joint CENG 318 / ID 322 course project in Spring 2026. The
project combines industrial design with computer engineering to produce a digital
companion application for a Bluetooth earphone product.

\begin{highlight}
Low-fidelity prototyping focuses on structure and flow rather than
visual polish. Materials are simple, such as paper sketches, pencil drawings, or
quick digital drafts, and iteration is fast. This makes it possible to test ideas
and make changes before any implementation effort begins.
\end{highlight}

\section{Project Objectives}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Develop a low-fidelity prototype of a Bluetooth earphone companion app UI
    for smartphones/tablets.
  \item Explore how users interact with the product's core functions.
  \item Emphasise usability, accessibility, and user experience principles.
  \item Document all design versions in order and justify changes between them.
\end{itemize}

\section{Product Vision}

The app allows users to connect, monitor, and personalise their Bluetooth
earphones from a mobile device. It is mainly designed for daily earphone users such
as students, commuters, and people who listen to music or podcasts while exercising.
These users often want to check battery level, connection status, and noise mode
quickly without searching through deep menus or taking the earphones out to inspect
them. For this reason, the app focuses on fast access to routine controls and clear
status information.

Core everyday tasks it supports:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Pair earphones quickly and see connection status at a glance.
  \item Check battery level without picking up the earphones.
  \item Adjust sound with one-tap presets or manual Bass/Mid/Treble sliders.
  \item Switch listening mode (Noise Cancellation / Normal / Transparency) in
    one tap.
  \item Manage account and device settings without navigating deep menus.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Design Rationale: Version 1 to Version 2}
% ============================================================

This chapter explains what was learned from Version 1 and which decisions
led to the changes in Version 2.

\section{Issue: Overcrowded Device Control Screen}

Version 1 combined the listening mode selector and a large multi-band equalizer on
a single device control screen. During review, this screen felt too dense. Users
who wanted a quick mode change had to visually compete with equalizer sliders, and
users who wanted sound adjustment had to look past the mode controls.

\begin{highlight}
Decision: The combined screen was split into two dedicated tabs, Sound for presets
and EQ sliders, and Noise for listening mode selection. This gave each function its
own clearer space.
\end{highlight}

\section{Issue: Two-Tab Navigation Was Too Sparse}

The two-tab structure, Devices and Profile, meant that the most frequent audio
actions were hidden behind a gear icon or a device card tap. This made the path to
common audio functions less obvious.

\begin{highlight}
Decision: The navigation was expanded to four tabs, Home, Sound, Noise, and
Profile, so the most frequent audio actions would stay close at hand.
\end{highlight}

\section{Issue: No Volume Quick-Access}

Version 1 had no defined interaction for changing volume quickly. Users were expected
to adjust volume without leaving the screen they were currently using.

\begin{highlight}
Decision: A volume overlay was added. It appears as a temporary modal capsule with a
percentage value and closes when the user taps outside it. This keeps the user in
the same context.
\end{highlight}

\section{Issue: No Usage Statistics Screen}

Version 1 had no way for users to see how much they had listened or whether they
were staying within safer listening levels. Feedback suggested that this information
would be useful.

\begin{highlight}
Decision: A statistics screen was added under the Sound area. It shows total
listening time, a historical chart with day, week, and month filters, and a safe
listening indicator.
\end{highlight}

\section{Issue: Preset-Only Sound Was Inflexible}

Preset buttons such as Bass Boost, Balanced, and Podcast gave quick options, but
they did not let users fine-tune the sound to match personal preference.

\begin{highlight}
Decision: A Custom preset was added. This opens Bass, Mid, and Treble sliders, and
a separate Save button stores the custom profile so changes are not made by accident.
\end{highlight}

\section{Issue: No Earbud Gesture Configuration}

Version 1 had no screen for assigning tap gestures, for example double-tap for
Next Song, to individual earbuds.

\begin{highlight}
Decision: Separate Left bud and Right bud screens were added under My Devices. Each
screen lists the available actions and shows the current selection with a checkmark.
\end{highlight}

\chapter{Design Reflection}
% ============================================================

\section{Key Design Decisions and Their Rationale}

\subsection{Home as the Central Hub}

Version 1 placed ``My Devices'' as the landing screen and did not have a clear
dashboard. The Version 2 nine-screen sheet introduced a Home dashboard that shows
connection status, battery, current mode, volume, and preset at a glance. This
reduces the number of taps needed for routine checks.

\subsection{Preset-First Sound Architecture}

Sound customisation is structured with presets first and sliders second. Casual
users can choose a preset in one tap, while users who want manual control can
select Custom and adjust the three-band EQ. The separate Save button prevents
accidental overwrite of a saved profile.

\subsection{Noise Mode as a Separate Tab}

Combining noise mode with the Sound tab was considered and rejected. Switching noise
cancellation on and off is a frequent and context-dependent action, for example when
entering a noisy environment or taking a call, so it was given its own tab instead
of being hidden inside another screen.

\subsection{Progressive Disclosure in Settings}

Settings uses a three-level drill-down:
General Settings $\to$ Device Detail $\to$ Automatic Power-Off.
Each level is opened only when the user decides to go deeper, which reduces the risk
of accidental changes to less frequently used preferences.

\section{Challenges}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Inconsistent tab labels across iterations.} Version 2 hand-drawn
    screens used Home / Sound / Settings / Profile, while Figma finalised Home /
    Sound / Noise / Profile. Resolving this required explicit agreement before
    high-fidelity work begins.
  \item \textbf{Density of the Version 1 device-control screen.} Deciding which
    controls belong on the main card and which should move to dedicated tabs took
    two review rounds.
  \item \textbf{Bluetooth state coverage.} Four distinct states, Empty, Searching,
    Connected Successfully, and Dashboard, each required their own wireframe to
    avoid unclear transitions.
\end{enumerate}

\section{User Considerations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item First-time users see only Add Device on an empty home screen, so the next
    action is clear.
  \item Returning users can change mode or preset in at most two taps from the
    Home dashboard.
  \item System states are shown explicitly, such as disconnected, searching,
    paired, and connected, instead of hiding these changes inside one screen.
\end{itemize}

\section{Accessibility Considerations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Frequently used controls were designed with large touch areas so they can
    be selected more easily on a phone screen.
  \item The top-level navigation was kept simple so users can move between main
    sections without memorising complex paths.
  \item Important system states, such as connection and pairing status, are shown
    clearly to reduce uncertainty.
  \item Common actions like checking battery level, switching noise mode, and
    selecting a sound preset are reachable in one or two steps.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

Over two design versions the companion app UI evolved from a minimal two-tab paper
prototype into a more structured four-tab interface with dedicated audio controls,
usage statistics, and earbud gesture configuration.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Version 1 established the main screen vocabulary, including the device list,
    profile and authentication area, and settings screens, using paper drawings and
    flowsheets to test transitions.
  \item Version 2 addressed the main gaps identified during review. The audio area
    was split into Sound and Noise tabs, the combined device-control screen was
    separated into clearer parts, volume control became a quick overlay, and
    statistics and bud controls were added.
  \item The overall design is based on the idea that frequent actions, such as
    pairing, mode switching, and preset selection, should be reachable in one or
    two taps.
\end{itemize}

The resulting structure is ready to move into the next phase of higher-fidelity
visual design and implementation.
